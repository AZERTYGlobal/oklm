# De jure Keyboard Standards for an Open Layout Model

## Scope and evidence base

For engineers building an open keyboard-layout model, the most important de jure reference family is **ISO/IEC 9995**, because it provides the cross-national vocabulary for **keyboard sections and zones, groups and levels, key numbering, and keycap imprints**. National standards then fill in the country-specific or language-specific allocations that ISO/IEC 9995 deliberately leaves to national usage or national standards. The public evidence base is uneven: some points are available directly from official ISO previews and national body catalogues, while many current standards are paywalled and expose only titles, abstracts, metadata, or tables of contents. Where that is the case below, I state so explicitly and avoid pretending that a marketing abstract is equivalent to the full normative text. A useful practical distinction is this. **Normative** material is the standards text itself: scope clauses, definitions, requirements, conformance clauses, and annexes marked normative. **Informative** material includes forewords, introductions, annexes marked informative, catalogue abstracts, press dossiers, blog posts, W3C overview sections, and vendor documentation. In the sources available publicly for this report, the clearest directly accessible normative text is the preview of **ISO/IEC 9995-1:2009**, especially its scope, conformance rules, and definitions; many newer ISO parts and most national standards are visible only through official catalogue metadata or summaries. The 2026 preview for ISO/IEC 9995-1 also shows that **Annex A is informative** and contains examples of national standards. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44view0Ņłéturn40search2Ņłéturn40search3Ņłü

Two points matter especially for system design. First, **ISO/IEC 9995-1 itself says that the primary group layout in the alphanumeric zone is typically established nationally**, while ISO/IEC 9995-2 provides allocation guidelines and ISO/IEC 9995-3 provides complementary layouts. In other words: the international series gives the vocabulary and framework; the national standards usually supply the country-specific primary arrangement. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44view0Ņłü

Second, **conformance is part-based, not all-or-nothing**. The accessible normative text in ISO/IEC 9995-1:2009 says that equipment conforms to Part 1 if it meets the relevant clauses; a keyboard claiming conformance with ISO/IEC 9995 must conform to **Part 1 plus all other relevant parts** for that model; and **any conformance claim shall list the parts** claimed. It also explicitly says that conformance to **Part 7** or **Part 8** does **not** require conformance to any other part of ISO/IEC 9995. That is exactly the legal-statement pattern an open model should mirror. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44view0Ņłü

## Physical geometry and position designation

In everyday engineering conversation, people say **ANSI**, **ISO**, **JIS**, and **ABNT** as if they were official geometry classes. That shorthand is useful, but it is not the most rigorous way to identify a key position. That same W3C overview makes a point that matters for the open model: many locale-specific hardware keyboards are not distinct physical standards at all, but are merely variants based on the existing **101/102** shapes with different keycaps and mappings. ## Legends, symbols, and multi-legend marking

For control keys and non-text functions, the crucial de jure source is **ISO/IEC 9995-7**. ISOŌĆÖs official page says it defines symbols used to represent functions on numeric, alphanumeric, or composite keyboards, and that each symbol is intended to serve as a **universal, non-language-related equivalent** of the function name. ISOŌĆÖs news release gives concrete examples such as Return, Escape, and Insert. As of June 2026, the catalogue still lists **ISO/IEC 9995-7:2009** as current, with **Amd.1:2012**, but also shows a DIS revision under development. For where legends go on a key and how several legends share one keytop, the relevant de jure anchor is **ISO/IEC 9995-1** itself. The public preview of the 2026 edition shows a dedicated legend section covering **general principles of key labelling and symbol positioning**, three named placement schemes ŌĆö **columnar placement**, **group-1-priorized placement**, and **level-4-including placement** ŌĆö as well as **capital/small letter pairs** and a **minimum required size of keytop symbols**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn40search2Ņłéturn40search3Ņłéturn40search4Ņłü

For text characters whose glyph alone is not self-explanatory on a keycap or in documentation, the complementary source is **ISO/IEC 9995-10**. ISO describes it as covering **conventional symbols and methods** for representing graphic characters that are **not uniquely recognisable by their glyph** on keyboards and in documentation. An open model that wants to standardise legend metadata should therefore distinguish two different standardised domains: **function symbols** from Part 7, and **character-representation conventions** from Part 10. ## Relationship to de facto OS layouts and to Unicode LDML Keyboard

Operating systems govern **implementation**, not de jure terminology. In Windows, the central object is the **keyboard layout** or **input locale identifier / HKL**. MicrosoftŌĆÖs API documentation says the input locale identifier specifies a **locale as well as the physical layout** of the keyboard, and Microsoft maintains a catalogue of available keyboard identifiers. MicrosoftŌĆÖs public layout pages also show that Windows ships concrete layouts implementing national standards, such as FranceŌĆÖs new standard AZERTY and GermanyŌĆÖs Extended E1. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn47search10Ņłéturn47search0Ņłéturn47search14Ņłéturn47search21Ņłü

macOS exposes a different implementation model. AppleŌĆÖs **UCKeyboardLayout** documentation says that the Unicode keyboard-layout (`'uchr'`) resource contains the data needed to map **virtual key codes** to **Unicode character codes** for a given layout, and **UCKeyTranslate** says it maps a combination of **virtual key code + modifier state** to Unicode output. That is an implementation data structure, not a de jure standard for keyboard sections, key numbering, or legend semantics. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn47search2Ņłéturn47search6Ņłéturn47search12Ņłü

**XKB** is the closest major OS-layer technology to ISO/IEC 9995 terminology. The X Keyboard Extension specification says that XKB makes most keyboard behaviour explicit on a per-key basis and adds the notion of a keyboard **group** to global keyboard state. Its protocol documents go further and say symbols are addressed by **keyboard group** and **shift level**, with ŌĆ£levelŌĆØ defined as in **ISO9995**. In practice, this makes XKB a very good interoperability bridge for an open model that wants its internal terms to line up with a widely deployed implementation stack. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn48search3Ņłéturn48search10Ņłéturn48search6Ņłü

**Unicode LDML Keyboard** sits in yet another layer. Unicode TR35 Part 7 says it specifies an **interchange format** for keyboard mapping data that is **independent of vendors and platforms**, so that one mapping file can be used across implementations. That overlaps strongly with the mapping and transform portions of a layout model. It does **not** present itself as a physical keyboard geometry standard or as a standard for keycap legend positioning; that limitation is an inference from its published scope as a vendor/platform-independent mapping-data interchange format. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn48search0Ņłéturn48search4Ņłéturn48search9Ņłü

So the clean division is this. **ISO/IEC 9995 and national standards** govern the vocabulary, geometry framework, legend conventions, and formal conformance vocabulary. **Windows/macOS/XKB** govern platform-specific implementation objects. **LDML Keyboard** governs portable mapping interchange. When these layers are confused, engineers end up re-inventing terms that ISO already standardised. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44view0Ņłéturn48search0Ņłéturn47search10Ņłéturn47search2Ņłéturn48search3Ņłü

## Implications for an open layout model

The open model should adopt ISO/IEC 9995ŌĆÖs core abstractions almost verbatim. The strongest candidates are: **section**, **zone**, **group**, **level**, **group-select / level-select**, **dead key**, and **key number / key reference position**. If the implementation also wants an engineering term such as ŌĆ£layerŌĆØ, that should be treated as an **alias mapped explicitly to ISO level or ISO group**, not as a replacement for them. That recommendation is especially strong because XKB already uses the ISO terms **group** and **level**, while ISO/IEC 9995-1 makes them part of the standards vocabulary.
